02 Foundation

What a landing page actually is (and isn't)

Most brands confuse landing pages with homepages or product pages. They're different tools for different jobs. Getting this wrong wastes ad spend and kills conversion before the visitor even reads the offer.

Page Type Primary Purpose Traffic Sources Navigation Typical CVR
Landing Page Single conversion action -- one offer, one CTA Paid ads, email campaigns, social ads None -- distractions removed 6.6%+ (industry median)
Homepage Brand introduction, multiple pathways Organic, direct, referral Full site navigation 2-3% for paid traffic
Product Page Product details, purchase decision support Organic search, internal navigation Site nav retained 3-5% (varies by category)
Collection Page Browse and filter a category Organic, paid broad match Full nav + filters 1-3%

When to use a landing page

Use a landing page when...
  • Running paid ads -- Google, Meta, TikTok, Pinterest. Every ad campaign needs a dedicated page.
  • Sending email campaigns -- A specific campaign (BFCM, product launch, seasonal sale) deserves its own page.
  • Promoting a single product or bundle -- Isolate the offer so nothing competes with it.
  • Testing a new angle -- Landing pages are the fastest way to validate messaging without touching the site.
  • Capturing leads -- Lead magnet, waitlist, quiz -- the action needs a focused environment.
Use your homepage / product page when...
  • Organic SEO traffic arrives -- These users are browsing, not responding to a specific ad.
  • Brand awareness campaigns -- Top-of-funnel content where no immediate action is expected.
  • Returning customers -- They already know you. Let them navigate freely.
  • General browsing -- Discovery traffic benefits from breadth, not a single forced CTA.
Key Rule

Message match is the #1 conversion driver before a visitor even reads the page. The headline on your landing page must mirror the copy in the ad or email that sent the visitor there. If your ad says "50% off running shoes" and the page says "Shop our collection" -- the visitor bounces immediately. Always match intent to message.

03 Anatomy

Every element of a high-converting landing page

A landing page is a system of parts that each do a specific job. Miss one or get the order wrong and the whole thing leaks conversions. Here's every zone, what it does, and what good looks like.

Zone 1 / Above Fold
Hero Section
The most important real estate on the page. Must contain: (1) Headline -- the clearest statement of your offer or value proposition, (2) Subheadline -- the "why it matters" in one line, (3) Hero image or video -- product in context, not on a white background, (4) Primary CTA button -- action-oriented text, contrasting colour, (5) Quick trust signal -- star rating + review count, or a short social proof line. Everything the visitor needs to decide to stay should be visible without scrolling.
Zone 2 / Below Fold
Social Proof Block
Dedicated section for evidence. Includes: (1) Customer reviews with name, photo where possible, and star rating, (2) Aggregate rating number ("4.8 stars from 2,400 reviews"), (3) User-generated content or before/after imagery, (4) Press logos or brand mentions ("As seen in..."). 60% of consumers trust UGC more than brand-created content. This section alone can move conversion rates by 15-25%.
Zone 3
Product / Offer Details
This is where you answer every objection before it arises. Covers: what the product is, what it includes, how it works, who it's for, and what makes it different. Use benefit-driven bullet points, not feature lists. Add a second CTA here. For higher-priced products, include a comparison table vs alternatives. For bundles, itemise what's inside and the total value vs what they pay.
Zone 4
Urgency / Scarcity Signal
Creates the reason to act now rather than later. Options: countdown timer (sale ends in...), stock scarcity indicator ("only 12 left"), limited offer (expires at midnight), or shipping cutoff ("order in the next 2h for delivery by Thursday"). Must be real -- fake timers reset and destroy trust permanently when caught. Scarcity tactics boost click-through rate by 9% and completed purchases by 7%.
Zone 5
Trust Signals
Removes purchase anxiety for new visitors. Includes: (1) Secure checkout badge (SSL / Shopify / Stripe), (2) Money-back guarantee (30/60-day), (3) Free returns or shipping policy, (4) Payment options (Buy Now Pay Later logos: Klarna, Afterpay), (5) Recognised certification or accreditation logos. Place these near the CTA or above the fold. Trust signals directly reduce cart abandonment.
Zone 6
FAQ Section
Handles the last objections before the decision point. Common questions: What's the return policy? When will it ship? Is this right for me? How does it compare to [competitor]? Will it work if [specific condition]? Write FAQs based on actual customer support tickets and pre-purchase questions -- not invented ones. Every answered objection on-page reduces pre-purchase abandonment.
Zone 7 / Bottom
Final CTA Block
Repeat the primary CTA at the bottom for visitors who scrolled all the way through. This should include: the main CTA button, a short one-line reinforcement of the key benefit or guarantee, and optionally a final trust badge cluster. Never end a landing page without a CTA -- a visitor who scrolls to the bottom has shown intent.

Above the fold -- the 8-second rule

Visitors decide within 8 seconds whether to stay or leave. Your above-the-fold zone must answer three questions instantly: What is this? Why should I care? What do I do next? Stores that show review count above the fold see 23% higher add-to-cart rates. Every element above the fold competes for attention -- remove anything that doesn't serve the conversion decision.

Remove These Immediately

Navigation menus / header links. Search bars. Sidebars. Related products carousels. Social media follow buttons. Chatbot widgets that pop on load. Every link or exit path you add to a landing page reduces conversions. A landing page with navigation leaks visitors back into the rest of the site before they convert. Remove the nav entirely.

04 Conversion Psychology

The science behind why people click buy

95% of purchasing decisions are driven by subconscious emotions, not rational analysis. Logic helps justify a decision after the fact -- emotion is what starts the action. These are the psychological levers that move e-commerce visitors from browser to buyer.

Cialdini's Six Principles -- Applied to Landing Pages

Principle 1

Social Proof

"If others like me are doing this, it's probably safe." Leverage: review counts, star ratings, UGC photos, real-time purchase notifications ("Sarah from Leeds just bought this"), aggregate buyer counts ("12,400 happy customers").

Principle 2

Scarcity

The fear of missing out (FOMO) is a stronger motivator than the desire to gain. Leverage: "Only 8 left in stock", limited-edition product runs, exclusive bundle pricing that expires. Must be genuine -- fake scarcity destroys brand trust permanently.

Principle 3

Urgency

Time pressure forces the decision now rather than later (where "later" usually means never). Leverage: countdown timers, sale end dates, shipping cutoffs ("Order in 3h 12m for next-day delivery"), flash sales with visible timers.

Principle 4

Authority

People trust experts, credentials, and recognition. Leverage: press mentions ("As featured in Forbes"), expert endorsements, certifications, founder credentials, brand partnerships, award logos. Authority turns scepticism into confidence.

Principle 5

Reciprocity

People feel obligated to return a favour. Leverage: free value before the ask -- a discount code, a free sample, a downloadable guide, free shipping threshold. Giving first increases the likelihood of receiving.

Principle 6

Commitment

Small yesses lead to bigger yesses. Leverage: quizzes that end in a recommendation, two-step checkout (email first, then payment), "Build your bundle" flows where the visitor invests time before seeing the price.

Copywriting Principles That Drive Conversions

Headlines

Your headline is the most-read copy on the page. It should contain the core benefit -- not the product feature. "Lose 10 pounds in 30 days" beats "Advanced fat-burning formula." Write it in second person ("You will finally...") or outcome-first ("Wake up energised. Every day."). Test headlines above everything else -- they move conversion by 15-50% on their own.

  • Outcome headline: Lead with the transformation, not the product ("The skincare routine that clears acne in 14 days")
  • Problem headline: Name the pain directly ("Tired of running shoes that destroy your knees?")
  • How-to headline: Promise a method ("How 4,300 runners fixed their form without physio")
  • Social proof headline: Open with the proof ("Rated #1 by 12,000 home gym owners")
CTA Copy

The button text is not decoration -- it's the last thing a visitor reads before deciding. "Buy Now" is the weakest possible CTA. Replace it with outcome-driven or first-person language that continues the visitor's thought.

  • Outcome CTAs: "Start Losing Weight Today" / "Get My Free Sample" / "Claim My 40% Off"
  • First-person CTAs: "Yes, I Want This" / "Send Me The Deal" / "I'm In"
  • Urgency CTAs: "Grab It Before It's Gone" / "Lock In My Price Now"
  • Low-friction CTAs: "See If It's Right For Me" / "Find My Size" (for hesitant audiences)

CTA button colour: must contrast sharply with the page background. Size: large enough to tap with a thumb on mobile without zooming. Position: above the fold AND repeated at the natural scroll stopping points.

Emotional Triggers

Emotion initiates the purchase. Logic justifies it. Write to both. Trigger the emotion first (aspiration, pain relief, belonging, status), then give the logical justification (ingredients, specs, the guarantee, the price).

  • Aspiration: Show the "after" state. Who does the customer become? ("Join 50,000 people who finally sleep through the night")
  • Fear of loss: What happens if they don't act? ("Every day you wait is another day of breakouts")
  • Belonging: Who else is in this group? ("Built for serious runners. Not for hobbyists.")
  • Status: What does owning this signal? Especially powerful for fashion, fitness, and lifestyle brands.
  • Relief: "You've tried everything else. This is different." -- especially powerful for problem-aware audiences.
Loss Aversion

Psychologically, people feel the pain of losing something twice as strongly as the pleasure of gaining the equivalent. Frame your offer as what the visitor is about to miss -- not just what they gain.

  • "Don't pay full price" beats "Save 30%"
  • "Sale ends in 4h 12m" beats "Sale on now"
  • "Only 6 left" beats "Limited stock"
  • "Your cart expires in 15 minutes" beats "Continue shopping"

Trust Signal Placement

Trust signals work hardest when placed at the point of maximum doubt -- which is usually just before or just after the CTA button. Don't bury them in the footer. A money-back guarantee placed directly under the "Add to Cart" button consistently increases conversion. An SSL badge in the checkout flow reduces abandonment. Placement is as important as the signal itself.

05 Mobile & Speed

The technical foundation that most brands ignore

83% of landing page visits happen on mobile. A page that looks great on desktop but loads slowly on mobile is a conversion killer. Technical performance is not a developer problem -- it's a revenue problem. These are the non-negotiables.

Load Time Targets

Speed Benchmarks
  • Under 1 second: 2.5-5x more conversions than 5+ second load times
  • Under 2 seconds: Google's recommended threshold for landing pages to rank well
  • Under 3 seconds: 53% of mobile users abandon pages that exceed this
  • Every 0.1s improvement: 8% uplift in retail conversion rate

Core Web Vitals

Google's Performance Signals
  • LCP (Largest Contentful Paint): Must be under 2.5 seconds. Your hero image is usually the LCP element -- optimise it first.
  • FID / INP (Interaction to Next Paint): Under 200ms. How fast the page responds to the first tap.
  • CLS (Cumulative Layout Shift): Under 0.1. Elements jumping around as the page loads destroys trust and causes misclicks.

Mobile-First Design Rules

Image & Media
  • Compress every image -- Use WebP format. Reduce hero images to under 150KB without visible quality loss. Tools: Squoosh, TinyPNG, Shopify's built-in compression.
  • Lazy load below-fold images -- Only load what's visible in the viewport on first render. Everything else loads as the user scrolls.
  • Use responsive images -- Serve mobile-sized images to mobile devices, not the full desktop image. Use srcset.
  • Video: autoplay short loops only -- For hero videos, keep under 10 seconds, muted autoplay, compressed MP4. Don't embed YouTube on landing pages -- it adds a 500ms+ delay.
Mobile UX
  • CTA button minimum 44x44px tap target -- Apple and Google's minimum recommended touch target size. Smaller = missed taps = lost conversions.
  • Single-column layout on mobile -- Never squeeze two columns onto a phone. Stack everything vertically.
  • Font size minimum 16px body text -- Smaller forces pinch-zoom which immediately degrades experience.
  • Sticky CTA bar on mobile -- A fixed "Add to Cart" or "Buy Now" bar that stays visible while scrolling. Average 18-32% conversion lift.
  • Short forms only -- Every extra field on a mobile form reduces completion rate. Name + email is the maximum for lead gen. Checkout: use Apple Pay / Google Pay where possible to bypass form entirely.
Technical
  • Remove render-blocking scripts -- Third-party scripts (chat widgets, pixel tags, survey tools) that fire on load delay the page. Defer non-essential scripts.
  • Use a CDN -- Serve assets from a server closest to the visitor. Shopify does this automatically. For custom landing pages, use Cloudflare.
  • Eliminate unnecessary apps -- Each Shopify app that runs on the page adds JavaScript weight. Audit regularly.
  • Preload hero image -- Add a preload link tag for the LCP image so it loads before the rest of the page completes.
  • Enable browser caching -- Returning visitors should load a cached version of unchanged assets.
Quick Win

Run your landing page URL through Google PageSpeed Insights right now. The "Opportunities" section will list the exact fixes ranked by estimated time savings. Focus only on the top 3. Those will deliver 80% of the speed improvement. Common top fixes: image compression, eliminate render-blocking resources, reduce unused JavaScript.

06 A/B Testing

What to test and what the data shows

A/B testing is the only way to know what works for your specific audience -- not for some average across industries. These are the highest-impact test categories, ordered by typical conversion lift, and real case studies showing what's been proven.

Test Priority Hierarchy

Priority 1
Headline
The single highest-leverage test. A winning headline alone can lift conversion by 15-50%. Test: benefit vs outcome, problem vs solution, question vs statement.
Priority 2
CTA Button
Test copy (verb-first vs outcome-first), colour (contrast vs brand), size, and position. Even small copy changes move conversion meaningfully.
Priority 3
Hero Image
Product alone vs product in use vs lifestyle vs founder. Emotion in the image vs clarity of product. Test one visual direction at a time.
Priority 4
Offer / Price Presentation
Discount framing ("50% off" vs "Save $40"), BNPL display, bundle structure, price anchoring (show original price crossed out).

A/B Testing Rules

  • Test one variable at a time. Testing multiple elements simultaneously makes it impossible to know what drove the result. The exception is multivariate testing with sufficient traffic volume (10,000+ monthly visitors).
  • Statistical significance is 95%+. Do not call a winner early. A test that looks like a winner at 78% confidence will flip 22% of the time. Use tools like VWO or Optimizely that calculate this automatically.
  • Minimum 1,000 conversions per variant. Small sample sizes produce noisy data. For low-traffic pages, run sequential tests (A then B) rather than split tests.
  • Don't test during anomalies. Holiday weekends, viral moments, and email blasts skew results. Run tests during "normal" traffic periods.
  • Document everything. The losing test is as valuable as the winner -- it tells you what your audience rejects. Build a test log so learnings compound over time.
  • Sticky Add-to-Cart benchmark: 18-32% average lift. If you have nothing else running, test this first on any product landing page.
07 Traffic Sources

Matching the right page to the right channel

A landing page built for a cold Facebook audience needs a completely different structure than one built for a warm email list. Traffic source determines intent, awareness level, and how much work the page needs to do before the CTA.

Cold Traffic (Paid Ads)

Meta, TikTok, Google Display, Pinterest

Visitor has no prior relationship with your brand. Awareness level is low. The page must do all the heavy lifting: establish credibility, explain the offer, build trust, overcome objections, and present the CTA. Longer pages typically outperform short ones for cold traffic.

  • Lead with social proof (not the product)
  • Include a founder story or brand origin section
  • Objection-handling FAQ is critical
  • Money-back guarantee must be prominent
  • Message must match the exact ad creative and copy

Warm Traffic (Email)

Email campaigns, SMS, push notifications

Visitor already knows the brand, trusts it, and opted in to hear from you. The page can be shorter, more direct, and assume familiarity. These visitors need less convincing and more urgency.

  • Lead with the offer and urgency (not brand explanation)
  • Reference the email ("As promised, here's your exclusive...")
  • CTA visible above the fold with minimal scrolling needed
  • Emphasise the exclusive / member-only nature of the deal
  • Shorter page length performs better for warm traffic

Intent Traffic (Paid Search)

Google Search, Bing Ads

Visitor searched for a specific term, meaning they have active intent. This is the highest-intent traffic source. The page must match the search query exactly (message match) and answer the specific need they typed in.

  • Headline must mirror the search keyword
  • Include dynamic keyword insertion where possible
  • Focus on why yours is better than search alternatives
  • Reviews and comparison content convert especially well here
  • Conversion from search landing pages averages 4-8%

Social Proof Traffic (Influencer/UGC)

Creator links, affiliate, TikTok Shop

Visitor was referred by a trusted creator or peer. They arrive with partial trust already established -- the creator provided the initial endorsement. Reinforce that relationship on the page.

  • Feature the creator's name or image if possible ("as recommended by [name]")
  • Mirror the angle the creator used in their content
  • Use a creator-specific discount code for tracking and perceived exclusivity
  • UGC-style content (raw, authentic) often outperforms polished studio imagery for this audience
Email-Specific Note

When sending email traffic to landing pages, the page URL matters for tracking. Use UTM parameters on every link (utm_source, utm_medium, utm_campaign) so you can attribute conversion accurately in Google Analytics or your store dashboard. Without this, all email-driven revenue appears as "direct" traffic and you lose the ability to optimise by campaign.

08 Build Guide

How to build a high-converting landing page step by step

A practical build sequence from blank page to live, tested, converting landing page. Follow this order -- skipping steps is how brands end up with pages that look good but don't convert.

Step 01
Define the single goal
One page, one conversion action. Buy, subscribe, or book. Never two. Write it down before touching design or copy.
Step 02
Map the traffic source
Who is arriving and from where? Cold ad traffic needs more trust-building. Warm email traffic needs urgency. The source dictates the structure.
Step 03
Write the copy first
Headline, subheadline, CTA text, bullet benefits, FAQ. Copy drives the wireframe -- not the other way around. Design should serve the words.
Step 04
Build the wireframe
Place every zone in order: hero, trust bar, benefits, social proof, urgency, FAQ, final CTA. No design yet -- just structure and copy.
Step 05
Design mobile-first
Start from the 390px viewport (iPhone). Single column. Large tap targets. Sticky CTA bar. Then adapt to desktop -- not the other way around.
Step 06
Optimise for speed
Compress all images to WebP under 150KB. Defer non-essential scripts. Test on Google PageSpeed Insights. Target 90+ on mobile before launch.
Step 07
Add tracking
Pixel, analytics, UTMs on every inbound link, conversion event firing on purchase/lead confirmation. No tracking = no optimisation data.
Step 08
Launch and record
Record a heatmap session from day one (Hotjar, Microsoft Clarity -- free). Watch real visitors. Drop-off points become your first test hypotheses.
Step 09
Run structured A/B tests
Start with headline. Wait for 95% significance. Implement winner, archive loser, document the learning. Repeat every 2-4 weeks permanently.

09 Ript Apparel — Landing Page Analysis

What Ript's pages actually do right now -- and what's broken

Ript doesn't have one landing page. They have four distinct page types that each function as a landing page for different visitors. The homepage, the individual design page, the Graveyard, and any paid traffic destination. Each one has specific gaps that are losing them conversions every single day.

Proven on Ript's Own Site

VWO documented a real A/B test run on riptapparel.com. They changed one thing: the CTA buy button. The winning version added urgency messaging directly into the button -- no new offer, no design overhaul, no price change. Just the button. Result: 6.3% increase in sales. This was their first ever A/B test. They ran one test, got a 6.3% lift, and there is no public record of them running another. Every gap below is a test they haven't run yet.

Page 1 -- The Homepage (their primary drop page)

Ript's homepage is effectively a landing page for their daily drop. It's where most visitors land and where the conversion decision happens. It currently shows 3 designs per day starting at 1am EST at $13-14 each.

What's broken

  • 3 designs competing for attention. Decision paralysis is real. When a visitor sees 3 options with no hierarchy, they pick none. There is no visual signal for which design is "today's best" or "most popular." Three equal CTAs reduce conversion -- this is documented to hurt by up to 266% compared to a single focused CTA.
  • No urgency per design above the fold. The countdown timer exists but its placement and visibility after the relaunch is unclear. The 24-hour window is Ript's single biggest conversion tool and it needs to be the first thing a visitor sees -- not something they find mid-scroll.
  • No purchase count social proof. "47 people bought this today" costs nothing to display and triggers urgency + social validation simultaneously. Their product is proven popular -- they just don't show it.
  • 1.7-star review average on third-party sites. Primarily shipping and customer service complaints. This isn't a landing page fix -- it's a trust problem that will undermine every other page improvement. No CRO tactic can overcome a broken post-purchase experience.

What to fix, in order

  • 1. Rank the 3 designs visually. Make one design dominant (larger, featured) and the other two secondary. The featured design is the "pick of the day" -- chosen by the team or by early vote data. Gives the page a clear hero without removing choice.
  • 2. Countdown timer above the fold on every design card. Not just on the product page -- on the homepage card itself. "Ends in 11h 22m" next to the Add to Cart. Every second the timer runs is a micro-urgency signal.
  • 3. Show purchase count on each design. Even "12 sold today" on a new drop tells the visitor this is real demand. Klaviyo or Shopify can surface this with minimal dev work.
  • 4. Fix fulfillment before anything else. A landing page fix on top of a 1.7-star fulfilment record is lipstick on a real problem. Reviews showing delayed shipping and no customer service response will override every conversion element on the page.

Page 2 -- The Individual Design Page (the core conversion page)

When a visitor clicks a design, they land on the individual product page. This is where the actual buy decision happens. Ript already knows urgency works here -- the VWO test proved it. The question is what else is missing.

Above Fold
Image slider starts with cropped art -- correct for this brand
Unlike most apparel brands where lifestyle imagery converts better, Ript sells art. The fan buying a Joy Division tee is buying the specific design, not the model wearing it. Showing the art first is the right call and should stay. What's missing: a "X people own this design" badge on the primary image, and a clear "today only" label that disappears at midnight.
CTA Zone
Urgency button tested and proven -- but never iterated on again
The VWO test proved urgency in the button copy works (+6.3%). That was the last documented test. The next test to run: first-person CTA copy. "Buy Now" vs "Get This Design Before It's Gone" vs "Grab Mine -- Ends Tonight." Each is a 30-minute test setup in any Shopify CRO tool. One of them will win by 10-20%.
Social Proof
Reviews exist but their position and volume is unclear
Reviews are present on product pages. The problem: each design is new every day, so no single design accumulates enough reviews to be persuasive. The fix is to show store-wide review stats above the fold ("4.6 stars across 12,000 orders") not design-specific reviews -- because no individual design will ever have enough. Brand-level trust works here, not product-level trust.
Size / Friction
Size selection is where drop-model brands lose sales
On a 24-hour drop, someone who can't quickly find their size in stock abandons rather than waits. A real-time size availability indicator ("XL -- 8 left", "S -- sold out") and a sticky Add to Cart bar on mobile would recover a meaningful portion of this abandonment. Modlet (footwear) added a size widget and saw +20.5% conversions and +68.9% revenue -- the principle is identical.
Below Fold
No artist story on the product page
Every Ript design is by an independent artist who earns royalties. That story is completely invisible on the product page. A short "About this artist" block -- name, one line bio, link to their portfolio -- adds a human layer to the purchase and differentiates Ript from every generic print-on-demand competitor. It also gives fans a reason to share: "I bought a shirt and supported an indie artist."

Page 3 -- Paid Traffic Destination (currently doesn't exist)

There is no evidence of Ript running dedicated landing pages for paid ads. If they run Meta or Google ads, those ads are almost certainly pointing to the homepage or a product page -- both of which have navigation menus, competing CTAs, and no message match to the ad.

The Gap

A Meta ad for a specific gaming tee design pointing to the homepage -- where that design competes visually with 2 others -- loses an estimated 37% of conversions vs. a dedicated page showing only that design with a stripped-down layout and a single CTA. For a brand running paid ads to a $13-14 product, that 37% gap is the difference between a profitable and unprofitable channel. Ript needs one dedicated landing page template for paid traffic that strips nav, shows one design, has the countdown front and centre, and ends with the buy button. Buildable in Replo or Gempages in a day.

What that page needs
  • No navigation bar. Every nav link is an exit. Remove it on paid traffic pages entirely.
  • Headline matches the ad. If the ad says "The gaming tee that sold out in 6 hours" -- the page headline says exactly that. Message match is the #1 conversion driver before a visitor reads anything.
  • Countdown timer at the top. Not mid-page. The first thing above the fold after the headline. "This design expires in 9h 44m" is the entire value proposition of Ript's model -- lead with it.
  • One design, one CTA. No carousels, no "you might also like", no other designs. The paid visitor came for one thing. Give them one decision.
  • Artist name visible. "Design by [Artist] -- they earn a royalty on every sale." One line. Converts browsers who care about supporting creators.

What is a lead magnet and when does e-commerce actually need one

A lead magnet is a piece of value you offer in exchange for an email address. For e-commerce, the most important word in that sentence is "value" -- not discount. Most brands treat lead magnets as a 10%-off bribe and wonder why their list is full of deal-hunters who never buy again. The brands winning at email list growth in 2026 are offering something their audience genuinely wants.

When to use a lead magnet in e-commerce

Use a lead magnet when...
  • Cold traffic is landing on your site -- First-time visitors aren't ready to buy. A lead magnet captures them before they leave so you can convert them over email.
  • You have a longer consideration cycle -- Higher-priced or niche products need multiple touchpoints. The list is your nurture channel.
  • You run paid ads -- Email capture from ad traffic costs less to convert the second time via email than re-targeting with paid again.
  • You have a drop or launch model -- Waitlists and early access lists are lead magnets that create demand before a product exists.
  • Your repeat purchase rate is low -- A lead magnet starts the relationship. The email sequence creates the repeat buyer.
Lead magnet types by conversion rate
  • Quiz / Personalisation tool: 20-40% opt-in rate. Highest performer. Converts because it delivers immediate, personal value.
  • Waitlist / Early access: 15-35% for engaged audiences. The VIP framing alone justifies the email.
  • Discount code (10% off): 3-8% opt-in. Most common. Least differentiated. Trains audience to wait for discounts.
  • Free shipping: Outperforms percentage discounts. 48% of consumers hand over their email for a discount; free shipping wins over price-off every time.
  • Checklist / Resource: 25-40% for the right audience. Only works when the brand has an educational angle.
The Problem With Discount Lead Magnets for Most Apparel Brands

A 10%-off popup on a $13 t-shirt gives away $1.30 and trains the customer to never pay full price. For brands already at aggressive price points, discounting the first purchase sets a dangerous expectation. The brands building the most valuable email lists in 2026 are not competing on discount -- they're competing on access, identity, and belonging.

Primary Recommendation: The Graveyard Revival List

Primary Lead Magnet

The Graveyard Revival List -- Subscribers join a community that votes to bring expired designs back to life. Once per month, the most-requested Graveyard design gets a 12-hour "One Night Only" revival drop. Subscribers get first access -- 2 hours before anyone else. The lead magnet is the power, not a discount.

Ript's Graveyard page is a live catalogue of every design that expired without the customer seeing it in time. The visitor who lands on the Graveyard is the hottest possible lead on the entire site -- they found a design they love and they missed it. That is the exact moment to capture their email. Not with a generic popup. With a system that turns their regret into participation.

How It Works
Step 1 -- Capture
On every expired design in the Graveyard: "This design is gone. But gone doesn't mean forever. Join the Revival List -- if enough fans vote to bring it back, it returns for one night only. You'll get 2-hour early access." Email only. One field. No discount.
Community Loop
Step 2 -- Engagement Email
Every subscriber gets a monthly "Vote to Revive" email showing the top 5 most-requested Graveyard designs. One vote per subscriber. This email has a clear purpose -- it generates intent data and the highest open rates in the calendar because subscribers asked to be part of it.
Revenue Event
Step 3 -- The Revival Drop
Once per month, the winning design returns for exactly 12 hours. Revival List subscribers get a 2-hour early access link. Everyone else finds out 2 hours later via the main list and social. The early access window creates real exclusivity without a single dollar of discount.
Artist Angle
Built-In Story
Ript pays artist royalties on every sale. A revival drop is a second royalty payment for an artist whose design already proved demand. This gives Ript a story to tell: "The community voted. The artist gets paid again. The design lives for one more night." That's content, not just commerce.

Why this is rare

What makes it different

  • No discount required. The value is access and voice. This protects margin and brand perception on a $11-$13 product where every dollar matters.
  • Uses an asset that already exists. The Graveyard page is live. This requires zero new content creation. Just a capture form and an email sequence.
  • Captures the highest-intent visitor on the site. Someone on the Graveyard page wanted to buy something and couldn't. That's not a cold visitor -- that's a pre-qualified buyer held back only by timing.
  • Creates segmentation automatically. Each subscriber is tagged by which design(s) they reacted to. When a revival drop runs, only fans of that specific design get the early access email. Open rates on targeted, intent-based emails typically exceed 50%.
  • The mechanic is self-reinforcing. The more people vote, the more the community feels ownership. Community ownership is what turns buyers into brand advocates who share the drops without being asked.

The data behind this strategy

  • Waitlist conversion to purchase: Subscribers who opted into a waitlist or early access list convert to purchase 30-50% on launch (vs 1-2% for generic email signups). These are buyers, not collectors.
  • Limited edition drops generate 300-500% higher margin than regular inventory when demand exceeds supply. A revival drop with proven pre-demand (votes) de-risks the production run entirely.
  • Intent-segmented email open rates: 50%+ when the subscriber explicitly asked for that specific notification. A fan who voted for the revival of their favourite design is going to open the "it's live" email. That's not a statistic -- that's basic human behaviour.
  • Artist royalty angle: Content with a human story (the artist) consistently outperforms pure product content in both organic reach and email click rates. Ript has this built in and currently isn't using it as an email hook.

Secondary Recommendation: Drop Insider Early Access

Secondary Lead Magnet

Drop Insider -- Email subscribers see tomorrow's design 6 hours before it goes live to the public. No discount. Just time advantage. Positioned as: "Never miss a drop again. Get first look, every day." This is a site-wide lead magnet (not Graveyard-specific) that works on the homepage, product pages, and exit intent.

The Drop Insider plays directly into the core emotional driver behind Ript's model: FOMO. Their entire value proposition is built on the fear of missing a design. The Insider list converts that fear into a reason to subscribe -- not because you save money, but because you gain the time advantage that ensures you never miss again.

Where it lives
  • Homepage hero: Embedded below the current daily drop countdown. "Tomorrow's design, revealed first. Join the Insider List."
  • Product page (on active drops): Below the Add to Cart. "Love this? Subscribers see what's coming before anyone else."
  • Exit intent popup: Triggered on tab close or back button. "Wait -- you'll miss tomorrow's drop. Get the first look, free."
  • Post-purchase: Confirmation page / email. "You just grabbed today's drop. Never miss another one -- join the Insider List." (Highest conversion point -- they just proved they buy.)

The Real Opportunity

Ript's drop model is already a conversion machine -- 24-36 hour windows, real scarcity, genuine FOMO. But every visitor who doesn't buy today and has no reason to come back tomorrow is a lost customer. The Graveyard Revival List and Drop Insider exist to solve exactly that -- they capture the people Ript's model was about to lose and give them a reason to stay in the relationship. No discount, no gimmick. Just time, access, and community.

Expected outcome based on comparable drop-model brands running waitlist/early-access lists: 25-40% email-to-first-purchase conversion within 30 days. Monthly revival drops projected to have 50%+ open rates and 15-25% click-to-purchase rates from engaged segments.
